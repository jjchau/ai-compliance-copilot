# POL-004-CONCENTRATION — CONCENTRATION RISK MANAGEMENT

Document Owner: Compliance Operations
Version: 2.0
Last Updated: February 2025
Internal Distribution: Advisory, Compliance, Branch Managers, Audit

---

# 1. Purpose

This policy establishes supervisory expectations regarding concentration risk within client portfolios.

Concentration risk exists when a portfolio becomes excessively dependent on:

* a single issuer,
* a single sector,
* a single asset class,
* or a limited group of correlated investments.

While concentration alone does not automatically indicate unsuitability, concentrated positions may expose clients to losses that are inconsistent with their documented risk tolerance, objectives, or financial circumstances.

Advisors are expected to assess concentration risk as part of all suitability reviews.

---

# 2. General Principles

Diversification remains an important risk-management tool for most retail investors.

Concentration reviews should consider:

* portfolio size,
* client net worth,
* investment objectives,
* investment experience,
* time horizon,
* liquidity requirements,
* and overall risk capacity.

Not all concentration is inappropriate.

Certain clients may knowingly maintain concentrated positions due to:

* employer stock ownership,
* legacy holdings,
* business ownership,
* inheritance,
* or specialized investment strategies.

In these circumstances, enhanced documentation may be required.

Where full portfolio data is unavailable, supervisors may consider transaction size relative to known financial circumstances, including annual income, assets, liquidity needs, or available account information, as a proxy indicator of potential overexposure.

---

# 3. Single-Issuer Concentration

Heightened supervisory review may be required when a single issuer exceeds a significant percentage of portfolio assets.

The following thresholds generally require additional review:

* positions exceeding 25% of portfolio value,
* positions exceeding 40% of portfolio value,
* concentrated speculative positions,
* concentrated positions in illiquid securities,
* or concentrated positions involving high-volatility issuers.

Supervisors may require additional rationale where concentration materially increases portfolio risk.

Large transactions relative to annual income or known liquid assets may require review where the exposure could materially affect the client's financial capacity, even if full portfolio concentration data is unavailable.

---

# 4. Sector Concentration

Advisors should consider exposure across economic sectors.

Examples include:

* technology,
* energy,
* financial services,
* healthcare,
* real estate,
* mining,
* and industrial sectors.

Sector concentration concerns may arise when:

* multiple holdings appear diversified but remain highly correlated,
* client portfolios become dependent on a single economic theme,
* or losses could reasonably be expected to occur simultaneously across multiple holdings.

Diversification assessments should consider correlation risk rather than security count alone.

---

# 5. Concentration and Client Objectives

Concentration risk should be evaluated in the context of client objectives.

Additional scrutiny may be required where concentrated positions exist alongside:

* capital preservation objectives,
* income-focused objectives,
* retirement income dependency,
* limited investment experience,
* low risk tolerance,
* or short investment horizons.

Concentrated speculative investments may be inconsistent with these profiles absent compelling documented rationale.

---

# 6. Documentation Expectations

Where material concentration exists, advisor notes should explain:

* why the concentration is appropriate,
* how risks were discussed,
* whether diversification alternatives were considered,
* and how the recommendation aligns with the client profile.

Documentation should specifically address:

* downside risks,
* volatility exposure,
* liquidity considerations,
* and client understanding.

Generic statements such as:

* "client likes the company,"
* "strong growth potential,"
* or "client requested purchase"

may be insufficient without additional context.

---

# 7. Escalation Triggers

The following situations typically require supervisory review:

* concentrated speculative positions,
* concentration exceeding internal supervisory thresholds,
* concentration combined with low investment knowledge,
* concentration involving elderly or vulnerable clients,
* concentration inconsistent with documented objectives,
* concentration in illiquid securities,
* or repeated concentration-related suitability alerts.

Escalated cases may require:

* additional advisor documentation,
* branch manager review,
* compliance consultation,
* or enhanced client confirmation.

---

# 8. Supervisory Review Notes

Concentration assessments are inherently fact-specific.

No single concentration threshold independently determines suitability.

Supervisors should evaluate concentration within the broader context of:

* portfolio construction,
* client circumstances,
* advisor rationale,
* and documented client understanding.

The existence of a concentrated position does not automatically indicate a policy violation.

However, failure to appropriately assess and document concentration risk may constitute a supervisory concern.
